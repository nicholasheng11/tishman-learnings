01 / Investing Vehicle

Opportunity Zone (OZ) Funds & Projects

A Qualified Opportunity Fund invests capital into economically-distressed areas ("Opportunity Zones") in exchange for capital-gains tax incentives. It's one of the strategies TCP runs on the investments side. I worked on understanding the underlying real estate projects inside the fund and brainstorming uses for their space.

What an OZ fund is

An investment vehicle that pools capital gains and deploys them into property/development in designated Opportunity Zones. In return, investors can defer and potentially reduce tax on those gains if they hold long enough.

The "90% Test"

A Qualified Opportunity Fund generally must keep at least 90% of its assets invested in qualifying Opportunity Zone property. It shapes what the fund can build or operate while keeping its tax benefits, so any new idea has to be OZ-compatible.

"Highest & Best Use"

The core question behind every site decision: of everything you could legally and physically put on a piece of land or space, which option earns the most relative to its cost and risk? A new idea has to beat the property's default use.

Two kinds of opportunity

Alt-Use = deciding what to build on vacant land (developer's job: more capital, zoning/permitting risk, longer timeline). Empty Commercial = deciding what fills an already-built vacant space (landlord's job: faster, cheaper, lower risk).

A project I worked on

I was tasked with researching three of the fund's properties and brainstorming the best uses for their available space, then producing a ranked one-page recommendation with rough cost ranges and trade-offs.

02 / Tax Strategy

Tax-Loss Harvesting (Quantinno DEALS)

Tax-loss harvesting is selling investments at a loss to bank a tax-deductible loss that offsets capital gains elsewhere, lowering your tax bill this year. Quantinno's DEALS platform is a leveraged long/short strategy built to manufacture those losses even in a rising market.

The concept, elementary

  • It defers tax, it doesn't delete it. When you sell a loser and rebuy something similar, your cost basis drops, so you'll owe a bigger gain later. You've pushed the tax into the future, not erased it.
  • Why deferral is still worth money: (1) you keep and invest the deferred tax dollars in the meantime, and (2) you can harvest losses at the high short-term rate (~40.8%) and repay later at the low long-term rate (~23.8%), a real rate arbitrage.
  • The wash-sale rule: you can't rebuy the same/identical security within 30 days or the loss is disallowed, which is why these managers hold many individual stocks, not one fund.
  • "Boot" & deferral ideas carry over to the 1031 world too (see below).

What DEALS actually is

  • A leveraged long/short strategy (e.g. "130/30" = $130 long and $30 short for every $100). The short positions are the trick: a loss on a short is a harvestable loss, so the strategy can generate losses even when the market rises, which plain long-only harvesting can't.
  • Three flavors: Core (fund with cash/portfolio), Overlay (use existing holdings as collateral, no new cash), Exchange (slowly diversify out of one big low-basis position).

What I took away, reading the marketing critically: A sales deck's "after-tax outperformance" number is mostly the credited value of harvested losses, not cash in your pocket. The benefit reverses on exit (an unwind tax bill), it only helps if you personally have big gains to offset, and leverage is real risk that gets framed as "more benefit." Every figure in a pitch deck is hypothetical/backtested, so treat it as marketing and verify independently.

03 / Hedge Fund

QFAF: Quantinno Fundamental Arbitrage Fund

A genuinely different product from DEALS. Where DEALS is a tax strategy that happens to use long/short, QFAF is a market-neutral long/short hedge fund trying to earn an uncorrelated return. The tax angle is secondary.

"Market-neutral"

Holds roughly equal long and short positions (~$2.50 long / $2.50 short per $1, ≈5x gross leverage) so net market exposure is ~zero. Designed to make money whether the market rises or falls, but it can still lose if the stock picks are wrong (it lost ~8.5% in 2020).

Where the return comes from

A daily multi-factor model ranks ~1,500–2,000 large U.S. stocks (value, momentum, quality, behavioral biases), goes long the good and short the bad. Profit is the spread between longs and shorts: pure stock-picking, not market direction.

The real selling point

Low/negative correlation (≈ −0.2 to −0.3) to stocks, bonds, and other hedge funds. Something that zigs when your other holdings zag is valuable even at a modest return, and that diversification is worth more than the headline number.

Hedge fund, not "hedge-fund-like"

Delaware LP, "2-and-20"-style fees (1.5% + 20% of profits), qualified/institutional investors only, leverage, short selling, prime brokers. Those are the defining features of a real hedge fund, all present.

What I took away, on the plumbing & the signals: Reputable service providers (KPMG = Big-Four auditor that independently verifies the money is real; HedgeServ = independent fund administrator that strikes the official NAV; prime brokers like J.P. Morgan / Morgan Stanley) reduce the risk of being defrauded, but they say nothing about whether the strategy will make money. Don't let prestigious names do more persuasive work than they should.

04 / Tax Strategy

1031 Exchanges (Background & Q&A)

A 1031 exchange ("like-kind exchange," IRC §1031) lets a real estate investor sell an investment property and roll the proceeds into another one without paying capital gains tax now. The tax is deferred, not erased.

The mechanics that matter

  • Real property only (since the 2017 tax law). No equipment or personal property.
  • "Like-kind" is broad: almost any U.S. investment real estate is like-kind to any other (an apartment building swaps fine for raw land).
  • The two clocks: 45 days from the sale to formally identify the replacement property, and 180 days to close. Miss either and the entire deferral collapses, with the full tax bill due. This is the central source of risk.
  • Qualified Intermediary (QI): must hold the sale proceeds; if the seller touches the cash, the exchange is dead.
  • Boot: any cash or non-like-kind value received is taxable. To fully defer, reinvest equal-or-greater value and replace the debt.
  • DSTs: Delaware Statutory Trusts let investors buy fractional interests in larger properties as their 1031 replacement, useful when a firm assembles ready replacement property for investors racing the 45-day clock.
  • PILOT: Payment In Lieu Of Taxes, a negotiated arrangement where a project pays a set amount instead of standard property taxes. Separate from 1031, but a common tool on incentivized real estate deals.

The big insight ("swap till you drop"): "Deferred" doesn't mean "paid later." A savvy investor can structure it so the tax is never paid. Keep exchanging until death, and heirs inherit with a stepped-up basis (basis resets to market value), and the deferred gain evaporates. And the investor can pull tax-free cash out by refinancing (a loan isn't a taxable event), rather than selling. One precision point: a taxable sale also triggers depreciation recapture (taxed higher than regular gains), which 1031 defers too.

§1031 survived the 2025 federal tax legislation intact, but it's a perennial legislative target.
05 / Technical Skill

Real Estate Pro Forma Modeling

"The Real Estate Pro Forma Modeling Master Class" by Justin Kivel. 17.5 hours, completed June 2026. A hands-on Udemy course on building dynamic real estate pro forma models in Excel from scratch, projecting a property's income, expenses, financing, and investor returns across a hold period.

🎓

Certificate of completion (Udemy, 17.5 total hours). Verify at ude.my/UC-5e57bc74…

What the course covered

  • Real estate finance fundamentals: how income property is valued and the time-value-of-money math behind every model.
  • Building a pro forma from scratch: modeling revenue (rents, other income, vacancy) and operating expenses up to NOI, the income engine of value.
  • Financing & the debt schedule: sizing a loan, amortization, and turning unlevered cash flow into levered cash flow to equity.
  • Investor returns: calculating and reading IRR, equity multiple, cash-on-cash, cap rate, and yield-on-cost together.
  • Sensitivity & scenario analysis: stress-testing the assumptions (rent growth, exit cap, hold period) that swing a deal's returns most.

Core concepts at a glance

NOI

Net Operating Income: rental + other income, minus operating expenses (before debt & taxes). The engine of property value.

Cap rate

NOI ÷ property value. The market's "yield" on a building; lower cap rate = pricier/safer asset.

Debt schedule

Loan amount, rate, amortization, and interest/principal over time. Turns unlevered cash flow into levered.

Cash-on-cash (CoC)

Annual pre-tax cash flow ÷ equity invested. "What I pocket each year."

IRR & equity multiple

IRR is the time-weighted return; equity multiple / MOIC is total cash back ÷ cash in. Shown together.

Exit / reversion

Projected sale value (often exit-cap × stabilized NOI), usually the largest single cash flow in the model.

07 / Technical Project

Full-Stack Web Project: Tennis Transfer Portal

A project assigned by my supervisors Chris and Katie: design and ship a live web application from scratch. I built a D1 men's tennis transfer-portal tracker, a searchable, sortable, filterable database of players entering the portal, and deployed it as a real, public website.

What it does

Tracks transfer-portal players with their previous and new school, status (In Portal / Committed / Signed / Withdrawn), ITA team rankings on both schools, and point-in-time UTR. Users can search, filter by status and conference tier (Power 4 / Mid-Major), and sort by recency, ranking, or UTR.

How I built it

A static site: a single index.html with inline CSS/JS, a transfers.js data file, and a custom add-player.html tool (single / smart-paste / bulk modes) that parses transfer posts into clean entries. Deployed free via GitHub Pages on a custom domain.

What I actually learned doing it

  • The Git/GitHub workflow: cloning a repo, committing, pushing, and deploying with GitHub Pages.
  • Working with an AI coding agent: how to write a tight, scoped prompt (list the files, specify exact values, set constraints, ask for a diff) so the work comes back right the first time, and when to chunk a task vs. do it in one pass.
  • Data modeling decisions: e.g. storing a point-in-time UTR "snapshot" instead of trying to keep live ratings current, and labeling class year by the season just finished to match the source data.
  • Product judgment: cutting features that don't earn their place (alphabetical sorts, redundant date lines) and resisting polish-on-polish in favor of validation: show it to a real user first.
  • Buying & pointing a custom domain at GitHub Pages (DNS A/CNAME records, HTTPS).
